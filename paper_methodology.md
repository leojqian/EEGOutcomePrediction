# Methods

## 2.1 Study cohort and outcome

The analysis cohort comprised 107 patients enrolled in a single-center randomized clinical trial of three treatments for chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy (CIPN)–associated pain: neurofeedback (NFB), duloxetine (DL), and their combination (NFB+DL). Each patient was randomized to one treatment arm at the baseline visit and rated pain unpleasantness on a 0–10 numeric rating scale (NRS) before treatment (T1) and after treatment (T2). All patients in the analysis had both a baseline resting-state EEG recording and paired T1/T2 ratings.

We defined two prediction problems (Figure 1). The first estimates a patient's baseline pain level from EEG and basic clinical information. The second — the focus of this work — predicts treatment response, so that likely responders and non-responders can be distinguished before therapy begins. For the treatment-response task we used the change in pain, ΔPain = Pain(T2) − Pain(T1), and dichotomized it at a clinically meaningful improvement of two NRS points: patients whose pain improved by more than two points were labeled improvers, the rest non-improvers. This produced an approximately balanced two-class split, so chance performance corresponds to a balanced accuracy of 0.50.

## 2.2 EEG and clinical features

Resting-state EEG was recorded from 19 scalp electrodes with the eyes closed for approximately five minutes. From each recording we derived quantitative features summarizing cortical activity in three families (Figure 2): spectral power across the standard frequency bands (delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma, and their sub-bands), functional connectivity between channels measured by coherence and the phase-lag index, and additional spectral descriptors such as total power, very-low-frequency power, peak frequency, and spectral entropy. Power and connectivity features were z-scored against a normative reference. Alongside the EEG features we used baseline clinical variables available before treatment: the patient's T1 pain rating, age, self-reported neuropathy duration, and the randomized treatment arm. Missing clinical values were imputed with the training-set median, and free-text neuropathy-duration entries were converted to numeric values.

## 2.3 Leakage-controlled pipeline

A recurring problem in EEG prediction studies on small cohorts is information leakage: when frequency-band selection, feature screening, or decision thresholds are tuned on the full dataset before the train/test split, reported performance is inflated and fails to replicate. We therefore built a deliberately conservative pipeline organized as three stages — split generation, per-fold band selection, and per-fold model fitting and evaluation — in which every modeling decision is made on training patients only. A runtime guard checked at every data-loading step and halted the pipeline if a held-out patient ever entered a training stage, and all random seeds were fixed for reproducibility.

The analysis used five-fold cross-validation. Patients were partitioned into five groups stratified jointly on the outcome label and on baseline pain severity (in terciles), so that each held-out group matched the others in both outcome balance and baseline-pain composition — this prevented the strongest single predictor, baseline pain, from drifting between training and validation folds. For each fold, the band-selection, model-fitting, and threshold-calibration steps were all re-run from scratch on that fold's roughly 85 training patients, and the resulting model was applied once to its roughly 21 held-out patients.

## 2.4 Band selection

Within each fold, we selected the most informative frequency bands separately for each EEG feature family (power, coherence, phase-lag connectivity). Using only the fold's training patients, candidate bands were scored by a repeated cross-validated tally: across seven patient-grouped folds, a penalized logistic-regression classifier was fit on each possible subset of candidate bands and the best-scoring subset (by balanced accuracy) cast a vote for the bands it contained. The two highest-voted bands per feature family were retained, yielding a compact set of six EEG bands per fold. This keeps the final feature count small relative to the cohort and ensures band selection never sees the held-out patients.

## 2.5 Model fitting and threshold calibration

For each retained band we summarized the corresponding EEG values by their mean and standard deviation across channels (for power) or channel pairs (for connectivity), giving twelve EEG features, which were combined with the baseline clinical and treatment-arm variables. On each fold's training patients we trained four model families — penalized logistic regression, random forests, support vector machines, and gradient-boosted trees — tuning each by grid search under inner cross-validation scored on balanced accuracy. We then compared the best single family against a soft-voting ensemble that averaged the four families' predicted probabilities, again using cross-validated out-of-fold predictions on the training set, and kept whichever performed better as the fold's final classifier. The decision threshold was calibrated on these out-of-fold training predictions rather than on the refit model's own training predictions, which avoids the optimistic bias that arises when a model fits its training data perfectly.

## 2.6 Evaluation

Model performance was assessed only on the held-out patients of each fold, the single point at which those patients were used. We report accuracy, balanced accuracy, area under the ROC curve, average precision, and F1, summarized both as the mean across the five folds and as a pooled estimate over all 107 held-out predictions. The area under the ROC curve was the primary endpoint, as it is independent of any particular decision threshold and directly reflects the model's ability to rank patients by their probability of response. We benchmarked the EEG-based model against two references evaluated under the identical cross-validation scheme: a majority-class baseline representing chance performance, and a clinical-only model that quantifies how much the EEG features add beyond readily available clinical information.
