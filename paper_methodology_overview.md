# Methods (overview altitude)

## Study cohort and design

We analyzed 107 patients enrolled in a single-center randomized clinical trial of three treatments for pain associated with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy (CIPN): neurofeedback, duloxetine, and their combination. Each patient was randomized to one treatment arm at the baseline visit and rated pain unpleasantness on a 0–10 numeric rating scale before treatment (T1) and after treatment (T2). All patients included in this analysis had both a baseline resting-state EEG recording and paired T1/T2 pain ratings.

From these data we defined two prediction problems (Figure 1). The first is to estimate a patient's baseline pain level from their EEG and basic clinical information. The second, which is the focus of this work, is to predict treatment response — the change in pain from baseline to post-treatment — so that likely responders and non-responders can be distinguished before any therapy is started. For the treatment-response task we classified each patient as an improver or a non-improver using a clinically meaningful pain-change threshold of two points on the rating scale, which yielded an approximately balanced split between the two groups.

## EEG and clinical features

Resting-state EEG was recorded from 19 scalp electrodes with the eyes closed for approximately five minutes. From each recording we derived a panel of quantitative features summarizing cortical activity (Figure 2): the power of brain oscillations across the standard frequency bands (delta, theta, alpha, beta, and gamma), the strength of functional connectivity between brain regions measured by coherence and phase-lag coupling, and additional spectral descriptors such as total power, very-low-frequency power, peak frequency, and spectral entropy. Alongside the EEG features we used a small set of baseline clinical variables available before treatment — the patient's baseline pain rating, age, self-reported duration of neuropathy, and the assigned treatment arm.

## Modeling pipeline

A recurring problem in EEG-based prediction studies on small cohorts is information leakage: when feature selection, frequency-band selection, or decision thresholds are tuned on the full dataset before splitting into training and test sets, reported performance is inflated and fails to replicate. We therefore built a deliberately conservative pipeline in which every modeling decision is made using training patients only.

The analysis used five-fold cross-validation. Patients were partitioned into five groups, balanced so that each held-out group matched the others in both outcome distribution and baseline pain severity. For each fold, the entire modeling procedure — selecting the most informative EEG frequency bands, choosing and tuning the predictive model, and setting the classification threshold — was repeated from scratch on that fold's training patients, and the resulting model was applied once to the held-out patients it had never seen. The held-out patients of a fold were never used in any decision that shaped its own model, and an automated check halted the pipeline if a held-out patient ever entered a training step.

Within each fold we first identified the EEG frequency bands most predictive of outcome, retaining a compact set of bands per feature type so that the final model used only a small number of features relative to the number of patients. We then trained several model families — penalized logistic regression, random forests, support vector machines, and gradient-boosted trees — and selected, by cross-validation on the training patients alone, either the best-performing family or a soft-voting ensemble that averaged their predictions. The decision threshold was likewise calibrated on training-set predictions. Keeping the feature count small relative to the cohort size, and fixing all random seeds, kept the models stable and the analysis fully reproducible.

## Evaluation

Model performance was assessed only on the held-out patients of each fold. We report accuracy, balanced accuracy, the area under the ROC curve, and related measures, summarized both as the average across the five folds and as a pooled estimate over all 107 held-out predictions. The area under the ROC curve was the primary endpoint because it does not depend on a particular decision threshold and directly reflects the model's ability to rank patients by their probability of response. We compared the EEG-based model against two reference points evaluated under the identical cross-validation scheme: a majority-class baseline representing chance performance, and a model using only the baseline clinical variables, which quantifies how much the EEG features add beyond readily available clinical information.
